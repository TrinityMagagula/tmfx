Lesson 3: Major, Minor, and Exotic Currency Pairs

üí± Understanding Currency Pairs

In Forex trading, currencies are traded in pairs. Each pair includes a base currency and a quote currency.

The base currency (on the left) is the currency you're buying or selling. The quote currency (on the right) is the one you're exchanging it for.

Example: If EUR/USD = 1.1000, it means 1 Euro is worth 1.10 US Dollars. If you buy EUR/USD, you're buying Euros and selling US Dollars. If you sell EUR/USD, you're selling Euros and buying Dollars.

üìò How to Read a Currency Pair

  • Buy = Long ‚Üí Expect base currency to strengthen against quote currency.
  • Sell = Short ‚Üí Expect base currency to weaken against quote currency.
Scenario: You expect the Euro to rise against the US Dollar. You go long on EUR/USD. If the price increases, you make a profit.
If you expect the Euro to fall, you short EUR/USD.

üåç Major Currency Pairs

Major currency pairs include the most traded currencies in the world and always involve the US Dollar (USD). These pairs are known for their high liquidity, tighter spreads, and relatively lower volatility, making them ideal for beginner and professional traders alike.

  • EUR/USD ‚Äì Euro / US Dollar
  • GBP/USD ‚Äì British Pound / US Dollar
  • USD/JPY ‚Äì US Dollar / Japanese Yen
  • USD/CHF ‚Äì US Dollar / Swiss Franc
  • USD/CAD ‚Äì US Dollar / Canadian Dollar
  • AUD/USD ‚Äì Australian Dollar / US Dollar
  • NZD/USD ‚Äì New Zealand Dollar / US Dollar
Example: If you believe the Euro will strengthen against the USD, you would buy the EUR/USD pair. If the EUR/USD rises, the Euro is gaining strength and you profit.
Another Example: If you expect the USD to weaken against the JPY, you would sell the USD/JPY pair.

ü™ô Minor Currency Pairs

Minor pairs, also called cross currency pairs, do not include the US Dollar but involve two other major currencies. They offer good trading opportunities but typically have slightly wider spreads and lower liquidity than major pairs.

  • EUR/GBP ‚Äì Euro / British Pound
  • EUR/JPY ‚Äì Euro / Japanese Yen
  • GBP/JPY ‚Äì British Pound / Japanese Yen
  • CHF/JPY ‚Äì Swiss Franc / Japanese Yen
  • EUR/AUD ‚Äì Euro / Australian Dollar
  • GBP/CHF ‚Äì British Pound / Swiss Franc
Example: If you expect the Euro to strengthen against the Pound, you would buy EUR/GBP. If the pair rises, the Euro has gained value and you profit.
Another Example: If you expect the Euro to weaken against the Australian Dollar, you should sell EUR/AUD.

üåç Exotic Currency Pairs

Exotic currency pairs combine one major currency with the currency of an emerging or smaller economy. These pairs tend to have low liquidity, high volatility, and wider spreads. Exotic pairs can be highly profitable but are also riskier and not recommended for beginners.

  • USD/TRY ‚Äì US Dollar / Turkish Lira
  • USD/ZAR ‚Äì US Dollar / South African Rand
  • USD/MXN ‚Äì US Dollar / Mexican Peso
  • EUR/SEK ‚Äì Euro / Swedish Krona
  • USD/THB ‚Äì US Dollar / Thai Baht
  • USD/HKD ‚Äì US Dollar / Hong Kong Dollar
Example: A trader might buy USD/ZAR if they expect the South African Rand to weaken due to political instability. If USD strengthens or ZAR weakens, the price of the pair rises.

üß† Key Takeaways

  • Major pairs are best for beginners due to high liquidity and tight spreads.
  • Minor pairs offer more volatility and opportunities with slightly higher costs.
  • Exotic pairs are high-risk, high-reward and require deeper knowledge, news awareness, and strict risk management.
  • Always consider spread costs, volatility, global news, interest rate decisions, and geopolitical events when trading currency pairs.
